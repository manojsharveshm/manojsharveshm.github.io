How Invoice Discounting is Quietly
Funding India's Content Boom

OTT platforms need content. Producers need capital. Invoice discounting is the bridge — and it's scaling faster than most people realize.

Manoj Sharvesh M Asset Operations · BetterInvest, Chennai

India produced over 3,000 hours of original OTT content last year. Behind every web series, every regional film, every reality show is a producer who needed working capital months before the content went live. The OTT deal might be signed. The satellite rights might be confirmed. But the cash? That arrives 90, 120, sometimes 180 days after delivery.

That gap — between a confirmed receivable and actual cash in hand — is where invoice discounting steps in. And it's quietly becoming one of the most important financing mechanisms in India's entertainment economy.

How It Actually Works

The concept is straightforward. A production house has a confirmed deal with an OTT platform — say, ₹5 crore for a web series. The contract is signed, delivery milestones are set, and the receivable is essentially locked in. But the producer needs money now — for crew salaries, location costs, post-production — not six months from now.

An invoice discounting platform steps in, verifies the contract and the counterparty, and advances a percentage of the receivable — typically 70-85% — at a discount. The producer gets working capital. The investor who funded the advance earns a return when the OTT platform pays up. The platform earns a fee for originating and managing the deal.

Invoice discounting doesn't create new risk — it monetizes existing certainty. The content deal is already done. The receivable is already confirmed. The only question is timing.

Why Entertainment Assets Are Interesting

Unlike traditional invoice discounting in manufacturing or services — where the counterparty might be a mid-market company with variable credit quality — entertainment receivables often have institutional counterparties. The debtor isn't a small business; it's Netflix, Amazon, Zee, or Sony. That changes the risk profile significantly.

The challenge is different: assessing the creative risk. Will the content be delivered on time and to specification? Will the production house manage its budget? These aren't credit questions — they're operational ones. And that's where the underwriting gets interesting.

The Scale Question

India's media and entertainment industry is projected to reach $100 billion by 2030. Regional content is exploding — Tamil, Telugu, Malayalam, and Kannada content is growing faster than Hindi. Every one of these productions needs financing, and traditional banks aren't set up to underwrite a web series the way they'd underwrite a factory.

Invoice discounting fills that structural gap. It's not trying to replace banks — it's serving a segment that banks can't efficiently reach. A ₹2 crore advance against confirmed OTT receivables doesn't fit neatly into any traditional lending category. But it's a perfectly sound financial transaction that creates value for everyone involved.

The most interesting part of this space isn't the financial engineering — it's the enabling effect. Every invoice that gets discounted is a film that gets made, a crew that gets paid, a story that reaches an audience. When the plumbing works, creativity flows. And right now, the plumbing is getting better fast.
