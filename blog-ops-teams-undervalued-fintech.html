Why Ops Teams Are the Most
Undervalued Asset in Fintech

Product gets the headlines. Growth gets the funding. But ops is what keeps the money moving — and nobody talks about it enough.

Manoj Sharvesh M Asset Operations · BetterInvest, Chennai

There's a strange hierarchy in fintech. Product teams get profiled in tech blogs. Growth teams get celebrated at board meetings. Engineering teams get the swag budgets. And then there's operations — the team that actually makes sure money moves from point A to point B without anything breaking.

Nobody writes blog posts about ops. Nobody tweets about their disbursement workflow. And yet, when ops breaks, everything breaks. A delayed disbursement doesn't just create a reconciliation headache — it erodes the trust that took months to build with an investor or a content producer waiting on funds.

The Invisible Infrastructure

In lending and alternative investment platforms, ops sits at the intersection of every function. Compliance needs ops to verify KYC before onboarding. Finance needs ops to reconcile every inflow and outflow. Product needs ops to flag edge cases that the system doesn't handle. Investors need ops to answer the question they actually care about: "Where is my money?"

The problem is that ops work is inherently invisible when done well. Nobody notices that 200 disbursements went out on time today. Everyone notices when one doesn't.

Good ops is like good plumbing — you only think about it when something leaks. But try building a house without it.

Why Fintech Ops Is Different

Traditional financial services ops is procedural — follow the checklist, file the report, escalate exceptions. Fintech ops is fundamentally different because the systems are still being built. There is no established playbook for disbursing funds against entertainment receivables or tracking repayments across 40 different production houses simultaneously.

Fintech ops people aren't just following processes — they're designing them. Every new deal structure, every new asset class, every regulatory update requires the ops layer to adapt. And because most early-stage fintechs don't have the luxury of dedicated process engineering teams, it's the ops person who figures out how to make it work.

The Automation Paradox

There's a common assumption that automation will eventually replace ops teams. It won't — at least not in the way people think. Automation handles the repetitive parts: generating documents, sending status updates, flagging overdue payments. But the judgment calls — deciding whether to escalate a delayed repayment, navigating a regulatory grey area, managing a relationship when things go sideways — those require human context that no workflow engine can replicate.

The best fintech ops teams use automation to free up time for the decisions that actually matter. The worst ones drown in spreadsheets and never get to the strategic work.

What Needs to Change

Three things. First, ops needs a seat at the product table. Too many features ship without ops input, creating downstream chaos that takes weeks to untangle. Second, ops metrics need visibility at the leadership level — not just "tickets closed" but "disbursement accuracy," "reconciliation speed," and "investor query resolution time." Third, ops careers need a real growth path. Right now, the best ops people either move into product or leave for consulting. That's a loss for the entire ecosystem.

Fintech is still young enough that these patterns aren't set in stone. The companies that figure out how to value, develop, and retain ops talent will have a structural advantage over those that treat it as a cost center to be minimized.
