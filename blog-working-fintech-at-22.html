What I Wish Someone Told Me About
Working in Fintech at 22

No MBA playbook prepares you for the pace, ambiguity, and builder mindset that early-stage fintech demands. Some honest reflections.

Manoj Sharvesh M Asset Operations · BetterInvest, Chennai
← Back to home

Nobody tells you that your first week at a fintech startup will involve simultaneously learning a new asset class, figuring out a spreadsheet someone built three iterations ago, and being on a call with an investor who assumes you've been doing this for years. You nod along, take notes, and Google things furiously during lunch.

That's not a complaint — it's the reality of working in early-stage finance, and honestly, it's the most accelerated learning environment I can imagine. But it would have helped to know a few things going in.

The Pace Is the Product

In a large company, your first six months are orientation. In a fintech startup, your first six months are a full career compressed. You'll touch operations, compliance, investor relations, product feedback, and probably some things that don't have a name yet because the company is too small for formal job descriptions.

This is disorienting at first. You spent four years in college learning theory, and suddenly you're in a world where the theory is being invented in real time. Invoice discounting for entertainment assets? There's no textbook chapter on that. You learn by doing, by asking questions that feel stupid, and by making mistakes that hopefully aren't expensive.

The most valuable skill in early-stage fintech isn't any technical expertise — it's the ability to figure things out without a manual. Comfort with ambiguity isn't a nice-to-have; it's the job description.

Nobody Cares About Your Degree

This sounds harsh, but it's actually liberating. In the first month, your B.Com or MBA matters for getting through the door. After that, what matters is whether you can build a tracker that actually works, communicate clearly with investors, and flag a problem before it becomes a crisis. Credentials are a starting point, not a ceiling.

The corollary is that learning never stops. Every new deal structure, every regulatory update, every product decision is a mini-education. The people who thrive in fintech are the ones who stay curious even when the daily work feels repetitive. The ones who struggle are the ones waiting for someone to teach them.

Relationships Are Infrastructure

In college, networking felt transactional — collect LinkedIn connections, attend events, exchange cards. In a working fintech, relationships are genuinely foundational. The production house CFO who trusts you enough to flag a delay early. The investor who calls you directly instead of sending an angry email. The colleague who explains the regulatory nuance you missed.

These relationships don't build overnight, and they don't build through formal networking. They build through reliability — showing up, following through, and being honest when you don't know something.

The Work Isn't Always Exciting

There's a version of fintech that gets written about in TechCrunch — funding rounds, product launches, industry disruption. And then there's the version you actually experience — reconciling payment data at 9 PM, drafting the same investor update for the third time because the numbers changed, and debugging a Google Sheet formula that's breaking your entire workflow.

Both versions are real. The glamorous parts are real. The mundane parts are also real, and they take up more of your time than anyone admits. The trick is finding meaning in the mundane — understanding that the perfectly reconciled ledger is what makes the investor trust possible, which is what makes the deal flow possible, which is what makes the company work.

◆ ◆ ◆

If I could tell my pre-job self one thing, it would be this: don't wait to feel ready. You won't feel ready for the first call, the first investor query, the first ops crisis. You do it anyway, learn from it, and feel slightly more ready the next time. That's the loop. It doesn't end — it just gets more interesting.
