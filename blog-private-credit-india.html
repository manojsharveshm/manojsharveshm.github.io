Private Credit in India: The ₹33 Lakh Crore
Opportunity Nobody's Talking About

Bank credit is tightening. NBFCs are cautious. Private credit AIFs are filling the gap at 12-18% yields — and the market is growing at 31% CAGR.

Something unusual happened in India's credit markets over the past two years. Banks, flush with deposits but cautious about asset quality, tightened lending standards. NBFCs, still recovering from the microfinance crisis, became selective about where they deployed capital. And into that gap stepped a category that barely existed five years ago: private credit.

The numbers are striking. Alternative Investment Fund commitments in India crossed ₹12.5 lakh crore in early 2025, growing at 31% CAGR. Private credit deal volume surged 35% year-on-year. And the yields — 12-18% annualized — are drawing capital from investors who've grown tired of volatile equity markets and suppressed fixed deposit rates.

What Private Credit Actually Is

Strip away the jargon and private credit is straightforward: it's lending by non-bank entities, typically through Category II AIFs, to mid-market companies that need capital but don't fit neatly into traditional bank lending criteria. The borrowers might be real estate developers between project milestones, manufacturing companies scaling production, or content producers with confirmed receivables but long payment cycles.

The structures vary — senior secured loans, mezzanine debt, revenue-based financing, invoice discounting — but the core logic is the same. The lender takes on credit risk in exchange for yields that significantly exceed what public fixed-income markets offer.

Private credit isn't a bet on growth. It's a bet on cash flow. The question isn't whether the borrower's business will triple in value — it's whether they can service their debt on schedule.

Why Now?

Three structural forces are converging. First, bank credit tightening. RBI's tighter provisioning norms and increased scrutiny of unsecured lending have made banks more conservative, pushing creditworthy borrowers toward alternative sources.

Second, the maturation of India's AIF ecosystem. Five years ago, private credit AIFs were a niche product for family offices. Today, they're mainstream — with established fund managers, track records, and distribution networks reaching HNIs and even affluent retail investors.

Third, the demand side. India's GDP growth, infrastructure push, and the explosion of new-economy businesses are creating borrowing demand that the traditional banking system simply can't absorb at the pace required.

The Risks Nobody Mentions

Private credit isn't risk-free, and anyone selling it as "fixed deposit plus" is being dishonest. The primary risk is credit default — if the borrower can't repay, the recovery process can be slow and uncertain, especially for unsecured or subordinated positions.

Liquidity risk is real too. Most private credit AIFs have 2-3 year lock-in periods. You can't redeem your investment when markets get scary — which is precisely when you'd want to. Concentration risk matters as well. A private credit fund with 15 deals is inherently more concentrated than a bond fund with hundreds of positions.

And then there's the transparency question. Not all AIFs report with the same granularity. Some provide detailed portfolio-level data; others offer opaque quarterly updates that tell you very little about actual asset quality.

◆ ◆ ◆

Private credit is becoming a core allocation in sophisticated Indian portfolios for good reason. The yields are attractive, the structures are improving, and the regulatory environment is maturing. But it's not a replacement for due diligence — it's a category that demands more of it. The investors who do well here will be the ones who understand what they're lending against, not just what return they've been promised.
